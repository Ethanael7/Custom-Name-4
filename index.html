The King of Settlement 4

Life on the Bucket

A backroad can look like a picture book—until you notice who the picture leave out.

Postcard Trinidad

Settlement 4 does shine like one of them primary-school illustrations—dusty road, savannah open wide, little life moving easy in the heat. People passing through will call it “simple.” They will say it sweet. But I living inside the frame, so I seeing the nice parts and the rot parts together, like they stitched with the same thread.

The omitted feature

Illustrators never draw the boy who planning to spend his teenage years perched on a paint bucket. They don’t draw the crate by the backroad, the quiet watching, the waiting. That is the other Trinidad too—the part where you learn early that you could be young and already feel finished.

“I ain’t no fool” — why school didn’t hold me

I didn’t tell myself I drop out because I stupid. I know I could talk proper when I have to, and I know I could do the work when the work real. But when the classrooms empty of teachers and full of noise, you start to measure value different. “Free education” sound good as a slogan; “free paycheck” feel like something you could hold.

Foster (and the gravity of following)

Foster was older, steadier—like he always had a plan even when the plan was just to disappear from school and reappear on the road. I didn’t like how easy it was for him. I did need reasons. I did need to feel like I was proving something to somebody. And when you hungry for a pattern to follow, you does fall in line behind the first person who walking like he sure.

The Village Castle

A housing block becomes a fortress when everybody agrees it is one.

Name the King, choose the side

In Settlement 4, power doesn’t come with a crown—it come with men willing to move when one man breathe. That is why people call him King like it’s a law of nature. If trouble ever decide to wake up and get busy, you want to believe you sitting on the winning side, even if “winning” is just surviving the week.

The chessboard — corners, blind spots, and bodies

He live high-up, and the rest of the fellas scatter out below like pieces on a board. Nobody does stand anywhere by accident. Every corner have eyes. Every blind spot have a shadow waiting. The place could look like pure neglect to outsiders, but inside it have order—dirty order.

Filth as infrastructure

The garbage, the loose boards, the overgrown yard—none of that is random. Mess does make pockets, and pockets does hide things. The uglier it look, the easier it is to tuck away what not supposed to be seen. You could call it stupidity if you watching from far; up close it’s planning.

Thursday morning — sirens, blue lights, and silence

Police does come like theater: sirens, lights, a little swagger in the morning sun. But everybody in the settlement know how that show does end. You could call it fear or loyalty, but I call it economy—nobody getting paid enough to dig through rust and risk their skin. When questions fall, voices does vanish.

The job — to sit, to watch

My work wasn’t running and shooting. My work was sitting. Sitting so long the road begin to feel like a classroom and the cars passing like lessons. People behind dark tints does judge you quick—like you wasting life. But from where I was sitting, they looked chained too, just chained cleaner.

Bodie and Bone — softness versus reputation

Boredom does rot the mind fast, so Foster bring a dog to make the hours feel human. The little dog was treated like royalty in a place where most dogs living rough. And then you had Bone—man hungry for a name, hungry for fear. In Settlement 4, respect does travel with violence, and plenty people willing to pay that price just to be seen as “big.”

The double life — textbook in hand, lookout in head

I wasn’t fully gone from school, not inside my skull. I start bringing a textbook like a secret—science by day, lookout by day too. I learn words like fulcrum and balance, and I wonder if a life could balance same way. But I still hide the book quick when a next corner boy pass. Pride is a funny thing: it does make you ashamed of your own escape plan.

Home pressure — and the walk back into danger

At home, my mother mouth could cut deeper than any blade. She tired, she angry, she calling me “big man” like it a curse. I leave the house and head back to the kiosk anyway, because outside at least the rules sound simple. And when the evening settle, you could feel something coming—like cold wind before rain.

The Cost of Power

Power doesn't ask permission—it takes, and it makes you thank it for the taking.

Foster and Bodie at the Kiosk

Foster used to bring Bodie to the lookout—a small brown dog, fluffy and old, who belonged to his brother Bailey. After Bailey pass, Foster kept that dog close like it was a piece of him still breathing. He would comb Bodie's fur, play with him, didn't care what people say. At the kiosk, Bodie was treated like royalty. In a place where most dogs living rough, that little fella had it good. But good don't last long when the King decide to make a point.

The King's Arrival

The King came to the kiosk that day smoking, wearing clothes old as him, grey stubble on his chin, horseshoe hair on his bald head. Foster and Bug froze. They hadn't expected him. Bone had been talking to the men outside while Foster waited inside, smoking weed he'd hidden in the floorboards. They thought they was safe. But safety is just a word people use before something bad happen.

"Ice Is What You Must Fraid"

The King extended his hand, voice smooth and strong. "Ice is what you must fraid," he said. Ice would freeze the world, preserving its ugly state forever. Ice mirrors would reflect from Russia to Trinidad, freezing everyone in their sins for eternity. "What sin you think you gon be frozen in?" he asked. The question hung in the air like smoke. Nobody answered. Nobody needed to. The King already knew.

The Killing of Bodie

"What you think the other Kings back in the day would do if they know you was stealing from them?" Foster said he didn't know, but the King already planned his demonstration. He looked at Bodie with murderous eyes. Foster begged him not to harm the dog, but the King was angry, and someone had to pay. He grabbed Bodie's leash, curled it around his fist. Foster started hyperventilating. Bodie lifted off the ground, kicking and barking wildly. The leash tied to a nail at the top of the kiosk. He hung there until he took his last breath. But the King wasn't finished. He took out his gun and shot the dead dog in its head.

Forced Gratitude

There was no one to help. Not a soul that didn't fear the King. The King knelt beside Foster, rubbing his back gentle like he was a child. "What's your name?" "Frederick Foster," he managed through tears. The King threatened him: he would be next if he ever interfered with the supply again. "Now, say thank you." "Thank me for my mercy." "Thank you." "For what?" "For your... mercy..." Power doesn't just take. It makes you grateful for what it leaves behind.

Balance

After the storm, you have to choose: do you clean the house, or do you become the storm?

Aftermath — Silence Between Foster and Bug

After the King left, Foster stayed on the road crying his eyes out. Bug had never seen him cry like that. He didn't know what to say—no experience with consoling others. He wanted to say something, but the words wouldn't come. A week passed. Bug still didn't know what to say. Sometimes silence is the only language trauma understands.

Cleaning the House

Bug finally decided to clean the house. On his knees, deep cleaning every corner. His kneecaps were dented by the time he was done. At three in the morning, he was sandpapering muck from the walls. He realized how much work went into housekeeping. His mom neither thanked him nor apologized. Neither did he. That was their family dynamic, and he was indifferent to it. Sometimes cleaning is not about cleanliness. It's about control. It's about making something right when everything else is wrong.

Refusal to Return

The next day, Bug decided he wasn't going back to the lookout. He would stop that work for good. But Foster got right back to it. Bug would walk past him every day, and they both refused to look at each other. Shame and defeat. Bug could admit he wasn't the right person for this job. Foster still held on. There wasn't much someone could do to stop that feeling, unless somehow they were able to shoot the person that made them feel that way. But Bug didn't have a gun. He just had a choice.

Bone's Promotion — What Staying Leads To

Bug noticed Bone had been promoted. From lookout/dealer to soldier of the fortress. He now had a cutlass buried in a bush and a gun hidden in a plank. Bone thought this meant no one could harm him now. No revenge, no comeuppance. He had all the King's men on his side. Nothing could be done, unless you didn't value your life. This is what staying looks like. This is what loyalty buys you. A cutlass in a bush. A gun in a plank. And the constant knowledge that someone out there might not value their life enough to fear you.

The Final Seesaw Scene

There isn't a true conclusion to this story. Bug didn't go back to school, yet he continued his personal studies. Yesterday, he went to the old broken-down playpark at the edge of the savannah. Pee-stained slides, faulty swings, a decapitated rocking horse. But the seesaw was working just fine. Two boys trying to play. One much chubbier than the other. They didn't know how to balance, so they started fighting. Bug stepped in. Calmed the children. Told the chubbier one to sit near the middle. Told the smaller one to sit further away. It finally balanced. The children had fun.

Load. Effort. Fulcrum.

Equilibrium. Balance. Advantage.

Bug understood physics now. He understood that balance isn't about equal weight. It's about knowing where to place yourself. Where to stand. The King had taught him that. Bodie had taught him that. Foster had taught him that. And now, standing in a broken playpark, watching two children laugh on a seesaw, Bug finally understood what he had to do.
